Moon phase is the part of solunar fishing that people latch onto first, probably because it's the easiest to see. You look up, the moon's full, and you figure the fishing should be good. It's not quite that simple, but moon phase genuinely matters, and once you understand how each phase changes the bite you can build a fishing calendar around it.

This is the practical version: what each phase does, and how to plan trips around the lunar cycle without overthinking it.

## Why Moon Phase Affects Fishing

Two mechanisms do most of the work.

**Gravitational pull and tides.** When the sun and moon align (new moon) or oppose (full moon), their gravity combines to produce the strongest tides, called spring tides. Bigger tidal movement means more water moving, more bait displaced, and often more aggressive feeding, especially in saltwater and tidal rivers. During quarter moons, the sun and moon pull at right angles, producing weaker neap tides and gentler movement.

**Moonlight.** A full moon floods the night with light, letting fish feed after dark. That can mean a slower daytime bite the next morning because fish already fed overnight. A new moon gives dark nights, which tends to concentrate feeding into daylight and the classic dawn and dusk windows.

Neither of these makes any single phase a guaranteed winner. They shift *when* and *how* fish feed, which is exactly what you want to plan around.

## The Phases, One by One

### New Moon
Dark nights, strong spring tides. Fish feed hard during the day because there's little nighttime light to hunt by. Major solunar windows around the moon's transit and anti-transit tend to be pronounced. Many anglers rank the days around the new moon among the best of the month, particularly in tidal water.

### Waxing Crescent to First Quarter
The bite settles into a moderate rhythm. Tides are weakening toward neap. This is a steady, dependable stretch rather than a peak. Dawn and dusk carry more of the load, and solunar timing still helps you pick the best hours.

### Full Moon
Strong spring tides again, but bright nights. This is the phase people overrate. The tidal push is real and can produce excellent fishing, but the nighttime feeding often means a sluggish mid-morning. The move on a full moon is to fish very early, very late, or even at night when legal and safe. Don't expect the same pattern as a new moon just because the tides are similar in size.

### Waning Gibbous to Last Quarter
Activity tapers as tides weaken back toward neap and nights stay fairly bright early on. Another moderate, workable period. Lean on solunar windows and low light.

The takeaway: the days surrounding new and full moons bring the biggest tidal movement, while quarter moons bring calmer conditions. New moon days often edge out full moon days because of the darkness factor.

## Building a Fishing Moon Phase Calendar

Here's how to turn all this into a plan for the month.

1. **Mark the new and full moons.** Block the two or three days on either side as your priority windows, especially if you fish tidal water.
2. **Layer in solunar ratings.** Phase is only half the story. Within those priority days, the moon's daily position still determines the strongest feeding windows. A daily 1 to 5 rating captures phase, position, and lunar distance together, so you're not just guessing from the calendar.
3. **Adjust for your fishery.** Saltwater and tidal rivers respond most strongly to phase. On a small lake, the tidal effect is negligible, so weight dawn, dusk, and weather more heavily.
4. **Plan full-moon trips for the edges of the day.** If your only free day is a full moon, fish first light, last light, or after dark rather than mid-morning.
5. **Stay flexible for weather.** A stable, mild stretch on a moderate phase can easily beat a cold front slamming through on a perfect new moon.

## Don't Overweight the Phase

The most common mistake is treating moon phase as the whole answer. It's one factor. A poorly-timed full moon day with a passing storm will disappoint no matter how good the tides look, and a "moderate" quarter-moon morning with stable weather and a well-placed solunar window can be excellent.

Use phase to pick your candidate days, use daily solunar ratings to pick your hours, and use weather to make the final call. ## FAQ

### What moon phase is best for fishing?
The days around the new moon are often best because strong tides combine with dark nights that push feeding into daylight. Full moons also bring strong tides but bright nights, which can slow the daytime bite.

### Is fishing better on a full moon or new moon?
Both bring strong spring tides. New moons usually have an edge because dark nights concentrate feeding into the day, while full moons often shift feeding to nighttime, leaving a slower morning.

### Does moon phase matter for lake fishing?
Less than for saltwater. Lakes have no meaningful tide, so the tidal mechanism barely applies. Moonlight still affects night feeding, but on still water you should weight weather, temperature, and dawn/dusk more heavily.

### How do I plan a fishing trip around moon phase?
Mark the new and full moons as priority windows, then use a daily solunar rating to find the strongest feeding hours within those days. Adjust for your water type and always check the weather before committing.

### How many days around a new or full moon are good?
Roughly two to three days on either side, since the strong spring-tide conditions build and fade around the exact phase rather than switching on for a single day.
